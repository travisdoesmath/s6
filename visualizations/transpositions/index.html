Permutations, Groups, Symmetry

When mathematicians talk about rearranging, we call it permuting, and we consider all possible rearrangements as permutations. We also have a way of organizing these permutations called groups. When you first learn algebra, you learn how to do (and undo!) operations on numbers like addition and multiplication. Groups are abstractions that capture the essence of these operations. With those abstractions, you can look at underlying patterns and structures and see where they might apply. This is part of why math can be so powerful; when we take something we're familiar with, abstract that structure and apply it to something unfamiliar, we can unlock new insights and understandings.

An semi-formal definition of a group

There is a formal definition of a group in abstract algebra, but here, we'll talk about it in more informal language

We'll consider a group to be a collection of actions that can be performed on some set of items. The actions must satisfy three properties:

  • Closure: for any two actions in the group, performing one after the other results in another action in the group.
  • Identity: there is an action in the group that does nothing
  • Inverses: for each action in the group, doing the action in reverse is also in the group

Let's consider these properties with a group we're already familiar with: the integers with addition.

  • Closure: adding any two integers always results in another integer
  • Identity: adding zero to any integer does not change its value
  • Inverses: for any integer, there is also the negative of that integer

Groups and Symmetry

Now, let's take that understanding of groups and apply it to a different context.

Consider a square. What are all the ways we can move it so that it looks exactly the same as when we started? We can:

  • Rotate it 90°, 180°, or 270° clockwise
  • Flip it horizontally or vertically
  • Flip it along either diagonal
  • Leave it as is (the identity transformation)

These eight transformations form a group! Let's verify the properties:

  • Closure: performing any two of these transformations always results in another transformation in our list
  • Identity: leaving the square as is (doing nothing) is in our list
  • Inverses: each transformation can be undone by another transformation in our list

We call these actions the symmetries of the square, and the group they form is called the dihedral group of order 8, often denoted D4. This is an example of a symmetry group, which is a group that describes the symmetries of a geometric object.

Groups are a natural way to understand symmetry.
